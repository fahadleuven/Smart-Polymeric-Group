Design and Synthesis of Hydrophilic Polymers for additive manufacturing

Poly(ε-caprolactone) (PCL) is the gold-standard material for melt electrowriting because it melts at low temperature, crystallizes rapidly, and provides excellent fiber fidelity and process stability. However, for many applications—particularly those that require interaction with water or biological interfaces—PCL is not suitable because it is intrinsically hydrophobic. Its low surface wettability limits moisture interaction, hinders functional coatings, and restricts its use in applications such as sensing or soft biological interfaces, where hydrophilic polymer networks are required.

Mahsa is working on designing and synthesizing hydrophilic, crosslinkable polymer systems that are compatible with melt electrowriting while overcoming these limitations. Her work focuses on adapting hydrophilic polymers, such as PEG-based systems, to meet the rheological and thermal requirements of MEW without sacrificing their water affinity. This includes tuning molecular weight, crystallization behavior, and crosslinking chemistry to enable stable fiber formation, followed by post-print curing to lock the microarchitecture. Through this approach, the goal is to expand the material palette of MEW beyond hydrophobic thermoplastics and enable the fabrication of functional, water-interactive fibrous networks for advanced sensing and biointerfacing applications.

Supervision: Promotor Prof. Veerle Bloemen , co-promotor Prof. Arn Mignon

Postdoc: Mahsa Ebrahimi
