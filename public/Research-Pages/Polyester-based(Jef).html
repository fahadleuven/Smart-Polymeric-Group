Polyester-based scaffolds for flexor tendon repair

There is an urgent need for the development of a construct that is equipped with sufficient mechanical properties, a controlled release of active compounds and finally minimizes scar tissue to repair flexor tendon injuries. Within this project, a polymer-based, double-layered tubular construct equipped with a stimuli-responsive drug release functionality is being developed. The finality of this project is to produce a construct that provides a controlled healing environment for injured flexor tendons. It is hypothesized that such a construct will reduce adhesion, tendon scar tissue and inflammation responses, and finally provide a solution for infections with controlled antimicrobial release.

Material-based constructs which wrap around the injury site have also been researched and developed extensively in the last decade. These constructs act as a mechanical barrier to prevent adhesion formation. In addition, such constructs are often processed using electrospinning (ES) to create a porous construct which enables the essential diffusion of nutrients and by-products produced by the construct biodegradation process (Figure 1).

Next to ES, there is another additive manufacturing technique offering a strong potential. Melt electrowriting (MEW) is an emerging technique in tissue engineering as well as in construct design intended for biomedical applications. It is a technique which uses thermoplastic materials to generate precise and defined microfibres and to build constructs with tuneable microarchitecture and external shape (Figure 2). In tendon repair, MEW would allow the generation of an inner construct layer where the fibres are aligned to the collagen fibrils in the flexor tendon. Alignment of the inner layer is deemed to enhance differentiation and cell proliferation, as well as provide the tendon cells with a topographical cue, guiding directional cellular growth.

If the flexor tendon sheath gets infected, the patient is of great risk of amputation of the finger due to the difficulty in treatment. In this regard, nanovesicles have gained increasing interest as drug carriers for biomedical applications (Figure 3). Especially polymersomes offer several advantages over other nanovesicles. Micelles only offer the possibility to encapsulate hydrophobic compounds while liposomes are often deemed less stable due to their thinner particle wall (3 nm vs. 10-50 nm for polymersomes). In addition, polymersomes have a higher loading capacity compared to the alternatives mentioned. The materials used for the development of these polymersomes can be chosen in a way that they become stimuli-responsive on triggers like temperature, enzyme, pH, light, etc.
