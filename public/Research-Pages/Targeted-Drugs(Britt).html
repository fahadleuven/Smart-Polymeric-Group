Colorectal cancer prevention using targeted drug delivery of anti-inflammatory drugs through reactive-oxygen-species responsive soft polymeric robots

Colorectal cancer (CRC) poses a significant problem socially and economically due to being the second biggest cause of cancer-related fatalities. Colorectal cancer often develops as a result of chronic intestine inflammation. As a society we wish to shift our healthcare from curative to preventative medicine. By tackling the inflammation in the intestine, the formation of CRC can be avoided.

Persistent intestinal inflammation can be countered using stimuli-responsive drug release. To overcome the limitations of stimuli-responsive nanoparticles, we propose the design of a polymer-based, double-crosslinked construct featuring a stimuli-responsive drug release mechanism.

People who have inflammatory bowel disease (IBD), which is a condition characterized by the chronic inflammation of the gastrointestinal tract, have a higher probability of numerous synchronous colorectal malignancies and are six times more likely than the general population to develop colorectal cancer. Large doses of immunosuppressive and anti-inflammatory medications are used in the standard treatment of IBD, but their low selectivity results in considerable side effects.

By combining a swelling layer and a non-swelling layer, a drug delivery construct can be made (see figure 1). The synthesized polymers have the unique ability to instigate swelling upon response to the stimulus in their environment. The mitigated swelling is used as an actuation mechanism to create systems that display fast movements. This creates a drug delivery system that only releases their cargo when inflammation or tumors are present, effectively increasing its selectivity.

Figure 1. Schematic overview of the deformations of different systems
that are envisioned, displaying (A) bending of a flat bilayer,
(B) rapid inversion of a curved bilayer, and
(C) the rapid expulsion of internal fluid of a drug delivery device.
